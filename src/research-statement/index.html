Research

I build and study human-centered AI systems that keep people safe online. My work spans the full pipeline of online safety — measuring risk, detecting abuse and fraud, and understanding how people interpret and trust the AI systems that increasingly make these decisions. I care about systems that work in the messy conditions of real platforms, not just in benchmarks.

Detecting harm in real-world conditions

Most risk-detection research studies harm in isolated, sanitized settings. My work insists on ecologically valid data and deployable methods. With youth as collaborators, I built datasets and models for detecting unsafe interactions in Instagram direct messages, grounded in young people's own lived experiences (CHI 2022, Honorable Mention; CSCW 2023, Impact Recognition Award). I showed that safety signals are increasingly multimodal — images and video, not just text — which matters as platforms move to end-to-end encryption and linguistic features alone fall short.

Abuse, fraud, and information integrity at scale

Harm is rarely confined to one account or one platform. My research traces coordinated and cross-platform abuse: how banned users migrate to less-moderated spaces after deplatforming (WebSci 2021), how state-sponsored trolls operate on Reddit and Twitter (IEEE S&P 2022; RAID 2024), and how online survey fraud threatens research integrity (SOUPS 2026). I also build adaptive, proactive detection that moves beyond brittle, lexicon-based moderation toward systems that evolve with the behavior they target.

Trustworthy AI as a human-centered problem

As AI systems take on more safety decisions, the harder question is whether people understand and trust them. My current work at TRAILS examines how people interpret AI-generated content, authenticity and provenance signals, and the visibility of AI use in shared and workplace settings. Safety, I've found, is not one feeling — people separate misinformation from offensive language, politeness from fairness, and trust from personal risk. Effective safety systems have to preserve those distinctions rather than collapse them into a single score.
